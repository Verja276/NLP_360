9.
- Approach the student privately and respectfully  
- Use open-ended questions (e.g., “How are you feeling?”)  
- Listen actively without interrupting  
- Show empathy and validate their feelings (e.g., “That sounds really hard”)  
- Avoid minimizing, blaming or giving immediate advice  
- Clarify concerns and ask about any immediate safety risks to self or others  
- Ask permission before sharing information or making referrals  
- Offer appropriate support options and referrals to services  
- Arrange a follow-up check-in or handover to support staff if needed  
- Maintain confidentiality except where safety requires disclosure

12.
- Match referrals to the specific nature and severity of the student’s distress  
- Prioritise immediate safety (crisis/emergency services if there is risk of harm)  
- Respect the student’s preferences (type of support, in‑person vs. online, confidentiality)  
- Consider cultural, language and accessibility needs  
- Direct to the most relevant service (counselling/mental health, GP/medical, academic advising, disability services, financial/housing/food support, peer support)  
- Provide clear contact details and how to access the service  
- Offer to assist with making the referral or follow up to ensure connection was made
